but what exactly is a period?

The menstrual cycle is a term to describe the sequence of events that occur in the body as it prepares for the possibility of pregnancy each month. The menstrual cycle starts on the first dayof the menstrual period until the first dayof your next menstrual period. The average menstrual cycle lasts 28days. Every person’s cycle is slightly different, but the process is the same.

let's start at the beginning...

At the beginning of the follicular phase, the lining of the uterus (endometrium) is thick with fluids and nutrients designed to nourish an embryo. If no egg has been fertilized, estrogen and progesteron levels are low. As a result, the top layers of the endometrium are shed, and menstrual bleeding, which often times is painful, occurs.

The medical term for period pain associated with menstruation is dysmenorrhea. It often begins a few hours before menstruation and persists for 2-3 days, with the pain strongest during the first 24 to 36 hours. The pain a person may feel can very from dull and constant to intense spasms. Dysmenorrhea affects 45 to 95% of people who menstruate.

Dysmenorrhea is caused by levels of prostaglandins, which are pain mediators. The number of them increase following the decrease of progesterone. Due to prostaglandins, spiral vessels, which supply the uterine lining with blood are no longer supplied with blood. As a result ischemia, which is painful, occurs. The uterus is deprived of blood and reacts by contracting to get rid of the uterine lining that is no longer useful, due to the egg not being fertilized.

nope, still in the follicular phase...

About this time, the pituitary gland slightly increases its production of follicle-stimulating hormone. This hormone then stimulates the growth of 3 to 30 follicles. Each follicle contains an egg. Later in the phase, as the level of this hormone decreases, only one of these follicles (called the dominant follicle) continues to grow. It soon begins to produce estrogen, and the other stimulated follicles begin to break down. The increasing estrogen also begins to prepare the uterus and stimulates the luteinizing hormone surge. On average, the follicular phase lasts about 13 or 14 days. Of the three phases, this phase variesthe most in length. It tends to become shorter near menopause.

The ovulatory phase begins with a surge in luteinizing hormone and follicle-stimulating hormone levels. Luteinizing hormone stimulates egg release (ovulation), which usually occurs 16 to 32 hours after the surge begins. The estrogen level decreases during the surge, and the progesterone  level starts to increase. The ovulatory phase begins when the level of luteinizing hormone surges. Luteinizing hormone stimulates the dominant follicle to bulge from the surface of the ovary and finally rupture, releasing the egg. The ovulatory phase ends when the egg is released, about 10 to 12 hours after the surge in the level of luteinizing hormone. The egg can be fertilized for only up to about 12 hoursafter its release.

the luteal phase

During the luteal phase, which lasts around 14 days, luteinizing hormone and follicle-stimulating hormone levels decrease. During most of this phase, the estrogen level is high.   Progesterone  and estrogen  cause the lining of the uterus to thicken more, to prepare for possible fertilization. If the egg is not fertilized, the corpus luteum degenerates and no longer produces  progesterone, the estrogen level decreases.

Premenstrual syndrome (PMS) is a combination of symptoms that arrive before a period, therefore, still in the luteal phase. Symptoms may primarily be physical, emotional or a combination of both. Either way, PMS symptoms disrupt life. The causes of PMS are still unknown.

It’s possible that hormone changes related to the menstrual cycle cause some people to experience PMS symptoms. Symptoms usually show up after ovulation, when the ovaries release an egg. The estrogen and progesteron levels dip around this time. Symptoms often go away a few days after the period, when hormone levels start to rise again. These hormone fluctuations may be to blame for PMS.
